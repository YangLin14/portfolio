Triton Droids is a UC San Diego student team building a humanoid robot from the ground up — hardware and software in parallel. I joined as an ML Team Member, working alongside the simulation and reinforcement learning sub-team to write the control policies that will eventually run on the physical robot.

Building From the Ground Up

The team splits roughly into two halves: a hardware team handling the mechanical design and electrical systems, and an ML team (simulation + reinforcement learning) writing the policies that control the robot. Because both halves are moving at once, the policies I write have to target a robot whose exact mechanical properties are still being finalized — there's no stable, fully-built platform to assume.

Current Projects

  • Falling policy in MuJoCo — training a policy so the robot goes down safely and cleanly when it loses balance, combining imitation learning with reinforcement learning rather than relying on hand-tuned recovery heuristics.
  • Panda arm pick-and-place pipeline — a simulation pipeline for a Franka Panda arm performing pick-and-place tasks, used as a second track for testing manipulation policies independent of the humanoid's locomotion work.
  • Humanoid Teleop — a wearable-IMU lower-body teleoperation pipeline built with a 6-person team as our UCSD CSE 145 final project, covering pose reconstruction, retargeting, and PPO motion tracking validated in IsaacLab and MuJoCo.

The Sim-to-Real Gap

What makes this different from most school robotics projects is that the hardware team is building the physical robot at the same time the ML team is writing the policies for it. The sim-to-real gap isn't an abstract case study read about in a paper — it's a concrete, recurring problem that shows up in nearly every team meeting, from mismatched mass/inertia estimates to actuator limits the simulator doesn't model yet.
